gsplat-grounding

Drop a character into a Gaussian-splat scene and have them walk on the visible floor — no authored collider, no preprocessing. The same .spz acts as both the visual environment and the collision source.

★ GitHub How it works API

Abstract

Gaussian-splatting captures look great but are notoriously hard to animate against — a splat is a cloud of fuzzy ellipsoids, not a manifold mesh. Today, putting a character into a splat scene means hand-authoring a collider in Blender, or relying on a separately shipped collision mesh.

This release is the first step in a longer arc: making characters aware of Gaussian-splat scenes the way they're aware of hand-authored worlds. A splat is a single bag of fuzzy ellipsoids; teaching it to behave like floors, obstacles, and interactive objects is the work.

V1 — this release — gives a character floor awareness. We sample where the splats are, figure out which ones make up the ground, and use that as the surface the character walks on. They follow slopes, climb raised areas, get stuck against things that protrude from the floor. While they're on their feet — walking, standing, idling — whichever part of their body is lowest snaps to that surface, so planted foot stays planted and swing foot lifts naturally. (Jumps and falls are the caller's concern: stop snapping during the airborne phase, resume on landing.)

The whole library is one short JavaScript file. It works with any splat the popular three.js splat renderer can read (.spz, .ply, .splat, .ksplat), and with any rigged character — Mixamo or otherwise.

What we tried — and what we found

Three options were on the table for "where does the character stand." Each one fails or succeeds for its own specific reason. The middle one explains why we couldn't just lean on what Marble Labs already provides; the third is what V1 ships.

approach 1 — collider mesh

Marble Labs ships a polygon collider mesh alongside every splat. Tempting to use it as the floor source — except that mesh is authored for first-person camera collision (don't fly through walls, don't fall through the world), not character grounding. Its lowest top-facing polygon at any (x, y) is whatever the camera could land on — booth seats, stool feet, table tops. A character grounded on it climbs onto furniture instead of walking the aisle.

approach 2 — no floor proxy

Pick a sensible constant Z by eye, plant the character there. No per-(x,y) lookup, no collision against anything. Beats the collider mesh for character navigation — at least the character doesn't climb seats. But the splat's floor isn't a perfectly flat plane: per-gaussian Z noise and reconstruction drift mean the visible surface ripples a few centimetres up and down. A constant Z misses those ripples; feet sink in the dips and float over the rises.

approach 3 — splat heightmap (V1)

Sample the splat itself for the floor. Per (x, y), gather nearby splats, sort by Z, then slide a 15 cm window through the bottom 60% of the sorted list and find the densest position. The upper edge of that densest window is the floor — it adapts to each scene's distribution rather than assuming the floor lives at any fixed percentile. The same .spz is the visual and the floor; nothing authored, no per-scene calibration for the common indoor case.

How it works

Step 1 — Read where every splat is

A Gaussian splat scene is, mathematically, a cloud of fuzzy ellipsoids. Each ellipsoid has a center point. We read those center points (about 30,000 of them) when the splat first loads. They outline every visible surface in the scene — the floor, the walls, the furniture, the ceiling.

Step 2 — Build the heightmap

For every cell, sort all nearby splats by Z, then slide a 15 cm window through the bottom 60% of the list. The upper edge of the densest window is the floor — adaptive to each cell's distribution rather than tied to a fixed percentile.

Step 3 — Each frame the character is grounded, snap them to the floor

For grounded animation — walking, standing, idling, sitting — we want one fact to stay true: whichever part of the body is lowest right now should be touching the floor. So each frame we:

  1. Look up the floor height at the character's current (x, y).
  2. Measure the lowest point on the character's body in their current pose.
  3. Lift or drop the whole character so that lowest point sits exactly on the floor.

The result: the planted foot stays put, the swing foot lifts naturally, and hips bob without the character ever floating above the ground or sinking through it.

Airborne animations — jumps, falls, dives, knockbacks — need the snap turned off during the airborne phase, otherwise the character would skid along the floor with feet always touching. The lib doesn't try to detect airborne vs. grounded automatically; the caller stops invoking groundCharacter() when the current animation isn't a grounded one and resumes on landing. Animation libraries (Mixamo, ActorCore, etc.) typically tag clips with this kind of metadata; if yours doesn't, a per-clip { grounded: true | false } flag in a small JSON next to your animations is the standard pattern.

three.js implementation notes

For a Mixamo-rigged SkinnedMesh animated by an AnimationMixer: update the mixer first so bones reflect this frame's pose, then take the precise bounding box — Box3.setFromObject(walker, true) — which transforms every skinned vertex into world space rather than reusing the rest-pose box. Bilinear-sample the heightmap at (walker.x, walker.y) for floorZ, then shift walker.position.z by floorZ − bbox.min.z.

Results

Tested on Marble Labs splats (indoor restaurants, halls, captures) and on mkkellogg's published demo scenes. Single-floor flat-ish indoor spaces are the strongest case. The library handles mild slopes in the captured floor by following them — the character traces the splat-derived terrain.

API

The library is one file. Five functions:

import {
  sampleSplatCenters,   // pull centroids out of an mkkellogg splatMesh
  buildSplatFloorHM,    // build the 2D floor heightmap once
  sampleSplatFloor,     // bilinear lookup at (x, y)
  groundCharacter,      // per-frame: snap walker to heightmap
  buildFloorCarpet,     // wireframe debug viz of the heightmap
} from './splat-grounding.js';

const samples = sampleSplatCenters(viewer.splatMesh, 30000);
const floorHM = buildSplatFloorHM(samples);

function tick() {
  mixer.update(dt);
  groundCharacter(walker, floorHM);
  renderer.render(scene, camera);
}

Strengths and limits

✓ works for
  • flat-ish indoor floors (rooms, galleries, restaurants)
  • outdoor terrain with smooth elevation
  • any rig three.js can bound (Mixamo, custom)
  • any splat format @mkkellogg/gaussian-splats-3d decodes
  • zero preprocessing, zero authored assets
✗ doesn't handle
  • airborne motion (jumps, falls) — caller stops snapping during the airborne phase
  • multi-floor environments (stairs, balconies)
  • walking under tables / overhangs
  • walls / lateral collision (only floor)
  • very sparse splats (<8 samples per cell)
  • scenes where rugs / cushions / very low furniture are the densest low-z band — distinguishing those from the actual floor needs object understanding (V4)

The single-floor limit is fundamental to z = f(x, y) — extending to stairs and balconies needs a multi-Z heightmap (store all dense z-bands per cell; walker picks the band closest to its current z). On the roadmap.

Roadmap

Floor awareness is the first piece. A character that knows where they can stand is a foundation for the rest: knowing what blocks their path, knowing what they can interact with. The roadmap below is what comes after V1.

V1 · this release

Floor awareness. Character knows where the ground is and follows changes in it — slopes, raised platforms, low obstacles that protrude from the floor. Kinematic snap: the lowest part of the body is forced onto the heightmap every frame.

V2 · next

Physics dynamics on the heightmap. Replace the kinematic snap with a one-sided collision against the heightmap, plus gravity on the character's pivot. Unlocks jumps, falls, knockbacks, dives — anything airborne. The heightmap stops being a constraint and becomes collision geometry.

V3 · later

Artefact correction. Splat reconstructions contain floating wisps and ghost ellipsoids that look like real surfaces but aren't. V3 distinguishes real geometry from noise so the blocked-space and walkable-space signals are clean.

V4+ · future

Object segmentation. Recognise the chair, the door, the jukebox as discrete objects. Build the bridge from "scene with stuff in it" to "things a character can sit on, open, pick up." Groundwork for character interaction in splat scenes.

Built on

  • @mkkellogg/gaussian-splats-3d — does all the splat decoding, sorting, and rasterization. We only consume splatMesh.getSplatCenter().
  • three.js — renderer, Box3.setFromObject(obj, true) (precise skinned bbox), AnimationMixer.
  • Mixamo — the demo character rig and walk animation (the actual .glb isn't shipped; bring your own).
